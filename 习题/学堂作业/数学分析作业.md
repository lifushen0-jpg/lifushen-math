## NO.1 

Suppose $B = B(x_0, r)$ is open in $\mathbb{R}^n$, and
$$
f : \overline{B} \to \mathbb{R}^n
$$
is a continuous map such that $f$ is $C^1$, injective, and non-degenerate on $B$.

Denote
$$
y_0 = f(x_0), \qquad S = \min_{|x-x_0|=r} |f(x)-y_0|.
$$

Prove that
$$
\overline{B}(y_0, S) \subset \operatorname{range}(f).
$$  

---

### Proof

Fix any
$$
y \in \overline{B}(y_0, S),
$$
that is, $|y-y_0| \le S$. We only need to show that $y \in f(\overline{B})$.

Consider the line segment from $y_0$ to $y$:
$$
y_t := y_0 + t(y-y_0), \qquad t \in [0,1].
$$
Define
$$
A := \{\, t \in [0,1] : y_t \in f(\overline{B}) \,\}.
$$
Since $y_0 = f(x_0)$, we have $0 \in A$, so $A$ is nonempty.

We first show that $A$ is closed. Let $t_k \in A$ and $t_k \to t$. For each $k$, choose $x_k \in \overline{B}$ such that
$$
f(x_k) = y_{t_k}.
$$
Because $\overline{B}$ is compact, passing to a subsequence if necessary, we may assume $x_k \to x \in \overline{B}$. By continuity of $f$,
$$
f(x) = \lim_{k\to\infty} f(x_k) = \lim_{k\to\infty} y_{t_k} = y_t.
$$
Hence $t \in A$, so $A$ is closed.

Next we show that $A$ is open in $[0,1)$. Take any $t \in A \cap [0,1)$. Then there exists $x \in \overline{B}$ such that
$$
f(x) = y_t.
$$
Since $t < 1$,
$$
|f(x)-y_0| = |y_t-y_0| = t|y-y_0| < |y-y_0| \le S.
$$
By the definition
$$
S = \min_{|z-x_0|=r} |f(z)-y_0|,
$$
every boundary point $z \in \partial B$ satisfies $|f(z)-y_0| \ge S$. Therefore $x$ cannot lie on $\partial B$, and thus $x \in B$.

Since $f$ is $C^1$ and non-degenerate on $B$, we have $\det Df(x) \ne 0$. By the inverse function theorem, there exist a neighborhood $U \subset B$ of $x$ and a neighborhood $V$ of $y_t$ such that
$$
f|_U : U \to V
$$
is a diffeomorphism. Because $s \mapsto y_s$ is continuous, for $s$ sufficiently close to $t$ we have $y_s \in V$, hence $y_s \in f(U) \subset f(\overline{B})$. So $s \in A$. Therefore $A$ is open in $[0,1)$.

Now let
$$
\tau := \sup A.
$$
Since $A$ is nonempty and closed, we have $\tau \in A$. If $\tau < 1$, then by openness of $A$ in $[0,1)$, there exists $\varepsilon > 0$ such that
$$
(\tau-\varepsilon,\tau+\varepsilon)\cap[0,1] \subset A,
$$
which contradicts the definition of $\tau = \sup A$. Hence $\tau = 1$, and so $1 \in A$.

Therefore
$$
y = y_1 \in f(\overline{B}).
$$
Since $y \in \overline{B}(y_0,S)$ was arbitrary, we conclude that
$$
\overline{B}(y_0,S) \subset f(\overline{B}) = \operatorname{range}(f).
$$

## No.2

Suppose $A$ is nonempty, both open and closed in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Prove that
$$
A = \mathbb{R}^2.
$$

---

### Proof

Choose any point $a \in A$, and define
$$
\widehat A := A-a = \{x-a : x \in A\}.
$$
Since translation preserves openness and closedness, $\widehat A$ is still a nonempty subset of $\mathbb{R}^2$ which is both open and closed. Moreover,
$$
0 = a-a \in \widehat A.
$$
Therefore it is enough to prove that
$$
\widehat A = \mathbb{R}^2.
$$

Now take any $x \in \mathbb{R}^2$.

If $x=0$, then clearly $x \in \widehat A$.

Assume now that $x \ne 0$, and set
$$
e := \frac{x}{|x|}.
$$
Then $|e|=1$ and
$$
x = |x|\,e.
$$

Since $\widehat A$ is open and $0 \in \widehat A$, there exists $\delta>0$ such that
$$
B(0,\delta) \subset \widehat A.
$$
Hence for every $t \in [0,\delta/2]$ we have
$$
|te| = t \le \delta/2 < \delta,
$$
so
$$
te \in B(0,\delta) \subset \widehat A.
$$

Define
$$
E := \{\, b \ge 0 : [0,b]e \subset \widehat A \,\}.
$$
Then $E$ is nonempty. Let
$$
\beta := \sup E.
$$
We claim that $\beta = +\infty$.

Suppose, for contradiction, that $\beta < +\infty$. By the definition of supremum, there exists a sequence $b_n \in E$ such that
$$
b_n \to \beta.
$$
Since $b_n \in E$, we have
$$
b_n e \in \widehat A
$$
for every $n$. Because $\widehat A$ is closed and $b_n e \to \beta e$, it follows that
$$
\beta e \in \widehat A.
$$

Now use the openness of $\widehat A$. There exists $r>0$ such that
$$
B(\beta e,r) \subset \widehat A.
$$
In particular,
$$
\left(\beta + \frac r2\right)e \in B(\beta e,r) \subset \widehat A.
$$

On the other hand, for every $0 \le s < \beta$, we can choose $n$ large enough so that $b_n > s$. Since $b_n \in E$, we have
$$
[0,b_n]e \subset \widehat A,
$$
hence
$$
se \in \widehat A.
$$
Together with $\beta e \in \widehat A$, this shows that
$$
[0,\beta]e \subset \widehat A.
$$

Because $B(\beta e,r) \subset \widehat A$, every point of the form $se$ with $\beta \le s \le \beta+r/2$ also lies in $\widehat A$. Therefore
$$
[0,\beta+r/2]e \subset \widehat A,
$$
which means
$$
\beta + \frac r2 \in E.
$$
This contradicts the definition of $\beta = \sup E$.

Hence $\beta = +\infty$. In particular, $|x| \in E$, and therefore
$$
x = |x|\,e \in \widehat A.
$$

Since $x \in \mathbb{R}^2$ was arbitrary, we conclude that
$$
\widehat A = \mathbb{R}^2.
$$
Therefore
$$
A = \mathbb{R}^2.
$$
\qed

## No.3

Let $P(z)=z^4+z^2$ for $z\in\mathbb{C}$, and identify $P$ with
$$
\widetilde P=\phi^{-1}\circ P\circ \phi
$$
on $\mathbb{R}^2$, where
$$
\phi \binom{x}{y}=x+iy.
$$
Prove that
$$
\operatorname{range}(\widetilde P)
$$
is closed in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

---

### Proof

For any $w\in\mathbb C$, consider the polynomial
$$
f_w(z):=z^4+z^2-w.
$$
This is a nonconstant polynomial with complex coefficients. By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, $f_w$ has a root in $\mathbb C$. Therefore there exists $z\in\mathbb C$ such that
$$
z^4+z^2-w=0,
$$
that is,
$$
P(z)=z^4+z^2=w.
$$
Since $w\in\mathbb C$ was arbitrary, we conclude that $P$ is surjective, and hence
$$
\operatorname{range}(P)=\mathbb C.
$$

Now $\phi:\mathbb R^2\to\mathbb C$ is a bijection, and
$$
\widetilde P=\phi^{-1}\circ P\circ \phi.
$$
Therefore
$$
\operatorname{range}(\widetilde P)
=\phi^{-1}(\operatorname{range}(P))
=\phi^{-1}(\mathbb C)
=\mathbb R^2.
$$
Since $\mathbb R^2$ is closed in itself, it follows that
$$
\operatorname{range}(\widetilde P)
$$
is closed in $\mathbb R^2$.

Thus the result is proved.

## No.1

Let
$$
\begin{cases}
x=r\cos\theta\sin\phi,\\
y=r\sin\theta\sin\phi,\\
z=r\cos\phi,
\end{cases}
$$
where $r>0$, $\theta\in(0,\pi/2)$, and $\phi\in(0,\pi/2)$.

Suppose
$$
f=f(p,q,s)
$$
is a $C^2$ function on $(0,\infty)^3$ such that
$$
f_{pp}+f_{qq}+f_{ss}=0.
$$

Define
$$
g(r,\theta,\phi)=f\bigl(x(r,\theta,\phi),\,y(r,\theta,\phi),\,z(r,\theta,\phi)\bigr)
$$
on
$$
(0,\infty)\times(0,\pi/2)\times(0,\pi/2).
$$

Find the corresponding equation for $g$.

---

### Proof

For simplicity, write
$$
p=x(r,\theta,\phi),\qquad q=y(r,\theta,\phi),\qquad s=z(r,\theta,\phi).
$$
Then
$$
g(r,\theta,\phi)=f(p,q,s).
$$

First compute the first derivatives:
$$
\begin{aligned}
g_r&=f_p\,p_r+f_q\,q_r+f_s\,s_r \\
&=f_p\cos\theta\sin\phi+f_q\sin\theta\sin\phi+f_s\cos\phi,
\end{aligned}
$$
$$
\begin{aligned}
g_\theta&=f_p\,p_\theta+f_q\,q_\theta+f_s\,s_\theta \\
&=-r\sin\theta\sin\phi\,f_p+r\cos\theta\sin\phi\,f_q,
\end{aligned}
$$
$$
\begin{aligned}
g_\phi&=f_p\,p_\phi+f_q\,q_\phi+f_s\,s_\phi \\
&=r\cos\theta\cos\phi\,f_p+r\sin\theta\cos\phi\,f_q-r\sin\phi\,f_s.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now compute the second derivatives.

Since $p_r,q_r,s_r$ do not depend on $r$, we get
$$
\begin{aligned}
g_{rr}
&=f_{pp}\cos^2\theta\sin^2\phi
+f_{qq}\sin^2\theta\sin^2\phi
+f_{ss}\cos^2\phi \\
&\quad +2f_{pq}\cos\theta\sin\theta\sin^2\phi
+2f_{ps}\cos\theta\sin\phi\cos\phi
+2f_{qs}\sin\theta\sin\phi\cos\phi.
\end{aligned}
$$

Also,
$$
p_{\theta\theta}=-r\cos\theta\sin\phi,\qquad
q_{\theta\theta}=-r\sin\theta\sin\phi,\qquad
s_{\theta\theta}=0,
$$
so
$$
\begin{aligned}
g_{\theta\theta}
&=f_p\,p_{\theta\theta}+f_q\,q_{\theta\theta}+f_s\,s_{\theta\theta}
+f_{pp}p_\theta^2+f_{qq}q_\theta^2+f_{ss}s_\theta^2 \\
&\quad +2f_{pq}p_\theta q_\theta+2f_{ps}p_\theta s_\theta+2f_{qs}q_\theta s_\theta \\
&=-r\cos\theta\sin\phi\,f_p-r\sin\theta\sin\phi\,f_q \\
&\quad +r^2\sin^2\theta\sin^2\phi\,f_{pp}
+r^2\cos^2\theta\sin^2\phi\,f_{qq}
-2r^2\sin\theta\cos\theta\sin^2\phi\,f_{pq}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Further,
$$
p_{\phi\phi}=-r\cos\theta\sin\phi,\qquad
q_{\phi\phi}=-r\sin\theta\sin\phi,\qquad
s_{\phi\phi}=-r\cos\phi,
$$
and hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
g_{\phi\phi}
&=f_p\,p_{\phi\phi}+f_q\,q_{\phi\phi}+f_s\,s_{\phi\phi}
+f_{pp}p_\phi^2+f_{qq}q_\phi^2+f_{ss}s_\phi^2 \\
&\quad +2f_{pq}p_\phi q_\phi+2f_{ps}p_\phi s_\phi+2f_{qs}q_\phi s_\phi \\
&=-r\cos\theta\sin\phi\,f_p-r\sin\theta\sin\phi\,f_q-r\cos\phi\,f_s \\
&\quad +r^2\cos^2\theta\cos^2\phi\,f_{pp}
+r^2\sin^2\theta\cos^2\phi\,f_{qq}
+r^2\sin^2\phi\,f_{ss} \\
&\quad +2r^2\cos\theta\sin\theta\cos^2\phi\,f_{pq}
-2r^2\cos\theta\sin\phi\cos\phi\,f_{ps}
-2r^2\sin\theta\sin\phi\cos\phi\,f_{qs}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Now consider
$$
L[g]
:=g_{rr}+\frac{2}{r}g_r+\frac{1}{r^2\sin^2\phi}g_{\theta\theta}
+\frac{1}{r^2}g_{\phi\phi}+\frac{\cot\phi}{r^2}g_\phi.
$$
Substituting the formulas above and collecting terms, we obtain:

- the coefficients of $f_p,f_q,f_s$ cancel;
- the coefficients of $f_{pq},f_{ps},f_{qs}$ also cancel;
- the coefficient of $f_{pp}$ is
$$
\cos^2\theta\sin^2\phi+\sin^2\theta+\cos^2\theta\cos^2\phi=1;
$$
- the coefficient of $f_{qq}$ is
$$
\sin^2\theta\sin^2\phi+\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta\cos^2\phi=1;
$$
- the coefficient of $f_{ss}$ is
$$
\cos^2\phi+\sin^2\phi=1.
$$

Therefore
$$
L[g]=f_{pp}+f_{qq}+f_{ss}=0.
$$

Hence the corresponding equation for $g$ is
$$
g_{rr}+\frac{2}{r}g_r+\frac{1}{r^2\sin^2\phi}g_{\theta\theta}
+\frac{1}{r^2}g_{\phi\phi}+\frac{\cot\phi}{r^2}g_\phi=0.
$$

This is exactly the Laplace equation in spherical coordinates.

## No.2

Prove that the equation $x^2 - 2xy + z + xe^z = 0$ uniquely determines an implicit function $z = f(x,y)$ in some neighborhood of the point $(1, 1, 0)$, and find the Taylor formula for $f(x,y)$ at the point $(1, 1)$ up to the second order.

### Part 1: Proof of Existence and Uniqueness of the Implicit Function

Let $F(x, y, z) = x^2 - 2xy + z + xe^z$. We need to verify the three conditions of the Implicit Function Theorem at the point $(1, 1, 0)$:

1.  **Verify the point is on the surface**:
    Substitute the point $(1, 1, 0)$ into the equation:
    $$F(1, 1, 0) = 1^2 - 2(1)(1) + 0 + 1 \cdot e^0 = 1 - 2 + 0 + 1 = 0$$
    The equation holds.
2.  **Verify the continuity of partial derivatives**:
    Calculate the partial derivatives of $F$ with respect to each variable:
    * $F_x(x, y, z) = 2x - 2y + e^z$
    * $F_y(x, y, z) = -2x$
    * $F_z(x, y, z) = 1 + xe^z$
    Clearly, these partial derivatives are continuous in some neighborhood of the point $(1, 1, 0)$.
3.  **Verify $F_z$ is non-zero at the point**:
    Substitute $(1, 1, 0)$ into $F_z$:
    $$F_z(1, 1, 0) = 1 + 1 \cdot e^0 = 2 \neq 0$$

By the **Implicit Function Theorem**, the equation $F(x, y, z) = 0$ uniquely determines a continuous implicit function $z = f(x, y)$ with continuous partial derivatives in some neighborhood of the point $(1, 1, 0)$, and it satisfies $f(1, 1) = 0$.

---

### Part 2: Finding the Second-Order Taylor Formula of $f(x, y)$ at $(1, 1)$

We need to find the first- and second-order partial derivatives of $z$ at $(1, 1)$. We know that at this point $x=1, y=1, z=0$.

#### 1. Calculate the first-order partial derivatives $z_x$ and $z_y$

Differentiate both sides of the original equation $x^2 - 2xy + z + xe^z = 0$ with respect to $x$ and $y$:

* **Differentiating with respect to $x$**:
    $$2x - 2y + z_x + e^z + xe^z z_x = 0 \quad \text{--- (Eq. 1)}$$
    Substitute $(1, 1, 0)$ (where $z_x = f_x(1,1)$):
    $$2(1) - 2(1) + z_x + e^0 + 1 \cdot e^0 \cdot z_x = 0$$
    $$2z_x + 1 = 0 \implies z_x = -\frac{1}{2}$$

* **Differentiating with respect to $y$**:
    $$-2x + z_y + xe^z z_y = 0 \quad \text{--- (Eq. 2)}$$
    Substitute $(1, 1, 0)$ (where $z_y = f_y(1,1)$):
    $$-2(1) + z_y + 1 \cdot e^0 \cdot z_y = 0$$
    $$2z_y - 2 = 0 \implies z_y = 1$$

#### 2. Calculate the second-order partial derivatives $z_{xx}$, $z_{xy}$, $z_{yy}$

* **Find $z_{xx}$**: Differentiate (Eq. 1) with respect to $x$ again:
    $$2 + z_{xx} + e^z z_x + (e^z z_x \cdot z_x + xe^z z_{xx}) = 0$$
    $$2 + z_{xx} + 2e^z z_x + xe^z(z_x)^2 + xe^z z_{xx} = 0$$
    Substitute $x=1, y=1, z=0, z_x=-\frac{1}{2}$:
    $$2 + z_{xx} + 2(1)\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right) + (1)(1)\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 + (1)(1)z_{xx} = 0$$
    $$2 + z_{xx} - 1 + \frac{1}{4} + z_{xx} = 0$$
    $$2z_{xx} + \frac{5}{4} = 0 \implies z_{xx} = -\frac{5}{8}$$

* **Find $z_{xy}$**: Differentiate (Eq. 1) with respect to $y$ (or differentiate Eq. 2 with respect to $x$):
    $$-2 + z_{xy} + e^z z_y + xe^z z_x z_y + xe^z z_{xy} = 0$$
    Substitute $x=1, y=1, z=0, z_x=-\frac{1}{2}, z_y=1$:
    $$-2 + z_{xy} + 1(1) + (1)(1)\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)(1) + (1)(1)z_{xy} = 0$$
    $$-2 + z_{xy} + 1 - \frac{1}{2} + z_{xy} = 0$$
    $$2z_{xy} - \frac{3}{2} = 0 \implies z_{xy} = \frac{3}{4}$$

* **Find $z_{yy}$**: Differentiate (Eq. 2) with respect to $y$:
    $$z_{yy} + xe^z(z_y)^2 + xe^z z_{yy} = 0$$
    Substitute $x=1, y=1, z=0, z_y=1$:
    $$z_{yy} + (1)(1)(1)^2 + (1)(1)z_{yy} = 0$$
    $$2z_{yy} + 1 = 0 \implies z_{yy} = -\frac{1}{2}$$

#### 3. Write the Taylor formula

The second-order Taylor formula for a bivariate function $f(x,y)$ at $(x_0, y_0)$ is:
$$f(x,y) = f(x_0,y_0) + f_x(x-x_0) + f_y(y-y_0) + \frac{1}{2!} \left[ f_{xx}(x-x_0)^2 + 2f_{xy}(x-x_0)(y-y_0) + f_{yy}(y-y_0)^2 \right] + o(\rho^2)$$

Substitute $x_0=1, y_0=1, f(1,1)=0$ and all the calculated partial derivatives:
$$f(x,y) = 0 - \frac{1}{2}(x-1) + 1(y-1) + \frac{1}{2} \left[ -\frac{5}{8}(x-1)^2 + 2\left(\frac{3}{4}\right)(x-1)(y-1) - \frac{1}{2}(y-1)^2 \right] + o(\rho^2)$$

Simplify to obtain the final second-order Taylor formula:
$$f(x,y) = -\frac{1}{2}(x-1) + (y-1) - \frac{5}{16}(x-1)^2 + \frac{3}{4}(x-1)(y-1) - \frac{1}{4}(y-1)^2 + o(\rho^2)$$
*(where $\rho = \sqrt{(x-1)^2 + (y-1)^2}$)*
